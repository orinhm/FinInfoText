# Learnings


- [2026-04-29] [2026-04-28] Energy supermajors (e.g., XOM, CVX) are operating with historically conservative capital structures (Debt-to-Equity < 25%) despite high commodity prices ($90+ WTI). Rather than aggressively expanding greenfield CapEx, they are prioritizing massive share repurchase programs and acquiring proven reserves (M&A).

- [2026-05-04] The WTI crude oil price surged from ~$85 to nearly $100/bbl in April 2026, driven by a convergence of Iran conflict escalation and the UAE's structural departure from OPEC+. This geopolitical premium is simultaneously compounding US inflation, with CPI reaching 330.29 in March 2026, threatening a deeper macroeconomic energy crunch.

- [2026-04-29] The UAE officially departed OPEC effective May 1, 2026, creating the first major structural fracture in the cartel since Qatar's exit. This removes ~3.2M bpd of production from OPEC's coordinated quota system, potentially flooding markets if the UAE maximizes spare capacity, but near-term uncertainty is net-bullish for crude.

- [2026-04-29] Iran-Israel tensions escalated through April 2026 with stalled nuclear talks and Strait of Hormuz naval posturing. Brent crude carries an estimated $15-20/bbl geopolitical risk premium. De-escalation would collapse this premium rapidly — a binary risk event for energy equities.

- [2026-04-27] The 'Sprott Shield' logic—where significant institutional positions (20%+) in a supply chain partner serve as a de-risking catalyst for the primary buyer—is a key signal for sentiment shifts in the industrial and energy sectors.

- [2026-04-27] Retail sentiment is shifting from pure EV plays toward the 'Energy Complex' (Copper, Lithium, Uranium) and electrical grid infrastructure necessary for AI data centers.

- [2026-04-29] [2026-04-28] During periods of heightened Middle Eastern geopolitical conflict, integrated supermajors can generate 'exceptional oil trading' outsized profits that buffer standard upstream/downstream operational margin compression.


## Uranium

# Learnings


- [2026-04-29] [2026-04-29] Middle East geopolitical conflict and blockades (e.g., Strait of Hormuz) create a hidden 'black swan' supply constraint for the uranium sector. Sulphur, a byproduct of oil/gas processing heavily shipped through the Middle East, is required to produce sulphuric acid—the critical chemical for In-Situ Recovery (ISR) uranium mining used by massive producers like Kazatomprom. Therefore, oil infrastructure disruption directly constraints ISR uranium supply, tightening the nuclear fuel cycle.
